# Emergent Learning

*Learning as dynamic, relational, and context-driven*

## What it is

Emergent learning is a pedagogical method that treats learning not as a one-way transfer of information from expert to learner, but as a dynamic process generated through the interaction between the learner, the content, the facilitator, and the context. It is rooted in Paulo Freire's critique of the "banking model" of education, complexity theory, and the learning sciences.

## What distinguishes it

The banking model deposits knowledge into passive learners. Emergent learning creates conditions in which knowledge arises from the learner's engagement with real problems in their real context. The curriculum is not the destination — it is the starting point.

Key features:
- **Context-driven adaptation** — the learning responds to the specific conditions of the specific learner in the specific moment
- **Collaborative inquiry** — learning is generated through thinking together, not transmitted from one to many
- **Iterative experimentation** — the learning system can change in response to what it finds; rigidity is a failure mode
- **Reflection in and on action** — Donald Schön's two modes of reflective practice, both of which are required for genuine adaptation

## What neuroeducation adds

Neuroeducational evidence supports emergent learning's core claims: active learning, spaced repetition, emotional engagement, and reduced cognitive load produce measurably better outcomes than passive content delivery. Neuroplasticity means the brain continues to grow and adapt in response to well-designed learning — but only learning that is designed to work with how the brain actually functions.
