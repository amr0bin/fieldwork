# WCAG 2.1

*Technical standards as necessary grammar*

## What it is

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1, developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), are the international technical standard for making digital content accessible to people with disabilities. In Canada, compliance with WCAG 2.1 Level AA is required under the Accessible Canada Act and the AODA. In the United States, the ADA's Title II now mandates WCAG 2.1 compliance for state-funded institutions.

## The four principles

- **Perceivable** — information and interface components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive. (Example: alt text for images, captions for video)
- **Operable** — interface components and navigation must be operable by all users. (Example: keyboard navigability, no seizure-inducing content)
- **Understandable** — information and interface operation must be understandable. (Example: plain language, predictable navigation)
- **Robust** — content must be robust enough to be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of assistive technologies.

## What WCAG is and isn't

WCAG is necessary grammar — the technical foundation without which nothing else is possible. A page that fails WCAG cannot be accessed by screen reader users, cannot be navigated by keyboard-only users, cannot be understood by users relying on plain language.

But WCAG is not sufficient. A page can pass every WCAG criterion and still be pedagogically useless, culturally alienating, or designed around assumptions that exclude specific learners. Technical compliance is the floor, not the ceiling.

# Universal Design for Learning

*Architecture, not add-on*

## What it is

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) is a framework developed by CAST (Centre for Applied Special Technology) that provides guidelines for designing learning environments that work for the widest possible range of learners from the start — rather than retrofitting accommodations for specific individuals after the fact.

## The three principles

- **Multiple means of representation** — providing information in more than one format so learners with different sensory, cognitive, or linguistic profiles can all access it
- **Multiple means of action and expression** — allowing learners to demonstrate what they know through different modalities (not only written text)
- **Multiple means of engagement** — designing for the range of learner motivation, interest, and self-regulation

## Why "architecture, not add-on"

UDL is often treated as a checklist of accommodations — add captions to this video, offer an audio version of that document. This is retrofitting, not UDL. True UDL means the multiple pathways are designed into the learning from the beginning, so no learner encounters a barrier that requires special accommodation to remove.

The architectural metaphor: a ramp added to the side of a building after construction is an accommodation. A building designed from the beginning so that every entrance is accessible to every person is architecture.

# Research Methodology

*Mixed methods, ethical practice, and the practitioner-researcher stance*

## The practitioner-researcher stance

Fieldwork is not academic research conducted by external researchers on practitioner populations. It is practitioner-research: inquiry generated from inside the practice, by the person doing the work, with and for the community the work is meant to serve. This changes the relationship between the researcher, the research question, and the participants — and it requires a different set of methodological commitments.

## Mixed methods

Fieldwork uses a mixed-methods approach that combines:
- **Qualitative methods** — interviews, focus groups, observational research, document analysis — to understand the lived experience of exclusion, hear what the structured questions didn't anticipate, and hold the specificity of individual stories
- **Quantitative methods** — surveys, institutional data analysis — to understand the scale and pattern of problems, test whether findings hold across a larger population, and produce evidence legible to institutional decision-makers

The two methods are not in competition. Quantitative data reveals the shape of the problem; qualitative data reveals what it is like to be inside it.

## Positionality

Positionality is the practitioner-researcher's honest account of their relationship to the research — their identities, their power relative to participants, the ways their perspective shapes what they can see and what they miss. Positionality is not a disclaimer; it is a methodological tool. Naming it makes the research more honest, not less rigorous.

## Ethical practice

Research ethics in Fieldwork is an ongoing relationship, not a one-time process. Key commitments:
- **Ongoing informed consent** — participants have the right to know how their data is being used and to renegotiate their consent if the research direction changes
- **Confidentiality and anonymity** — participants are protected, and the protections are explained transparently
- **Non-extractive practice** — research does not simply take what it needs from participants; it asks what participants and their communities need from the inquiry
