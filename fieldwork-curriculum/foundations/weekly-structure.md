# Weekly Structure

The recurring architecture of every week — and how the parts hold together

## The four elements every week contains

- **Heading — the provocation**

  A single statement or question that names the tension the week is working with. Not a topic label — an argument starter. Something that could be wrong, or surprising, or that challenges an assumption the reader is likely to hold. Write three options and choose the most unsettling.

- **Subheadings — the three moves**

  Every week makes three moves that mirror the three pillars: first grounding the reader in what the system currently does or requires (accessibility / infrastructure); then asking whose interests that serves and who it misses (disability justice / ethics); then opening toward what becomes possible when the design is made adaptive and relational (emergent learning / method). The subheadings name each of those moves.

- **Keywords — the conceptual anchors**

  Three to five terms that carry the week's intellectual weight and connect to the broader hypothesis. These recur across weeks in different combinations — this is how the curriculum builds cumulative understanding rather than isolated lessons.

- **Key sentences — the load-bearing claims**

  One sentence per subheading that a reader could extract and hold as the essential argument of that section. These are the sentences you'd underline. They should be true at the week's specific level and also true at the level of the whole hypothesis.

## The two-part post structure

**Part 1 — Why, See**

Grounded in what is. Understanding before action.

→ Why (250–350 words)  
→ See: Context  
→ See: What they did  
→ See: What changed  
→ See: What it reveals  
→ A question to carry into next week

**Part 2 — Try, Share, Reflect**

The practice. Moving from understanding to action.

→ Threshold sentence  
→ Try (paragraph + steps)  
→ Watch (video + context)  
→ Share (prompt for peers)  
→ Reflect (open question for the field)  
→ Next week preview

## The three moves in detail

Every section of every post makes one of these three moves. Use them to check your work:

**Move 1 — Ground**

What does the system, institution, or field currently do? What is the infrastructure or assumption? Open from inside the reader's existing reality before reframing it.

**Move 2 — Whose interests?**

Who does the current design serve? Who does it miss? Disability justice, access, ethics — not as lecture but as honest question from inside the practice.

**Move 3 — What becomes possible**

When the design is adaptive and relational, what opens up? What does the reader now have permission to do, change, or design differently? This is the sentence they'll remember.

## How the parts hold together across 48 posts

The 48 posts (24 weeks × 2 parts) are not standalone pieces. They form a cumulative argument. Part 1 introduces the concept; Part 2 puts it to work in the reader's context. The keywords from Part 1 should echo in Part 2's activity. The closing question from Part 1 should be the implicit question that makes Part 2's practice feel necessary. The Reflect section of Part 2 should point one step ahead of where the curriculum currently is. Each post should feel complete and also like part of something larger.
