# Working Hypothesis

The core argument Fieldwork is testing and building through 24 weeks

## The hypothesis

Sustainable and effective learning systems are only possible when they move beyond mere legal compliance (accessibility) to embody a radical, intersectional framework (disability justice) that facilitates continuous, context-driven adaptation (emergent learning).

## Full statement

By synthesising the three pillars — accessibility as infrastructure, disability justice as ethical compass, and emergent learning as pedagogical method — inclusive learning designers can build systems that are not just legally compliant but genuinely inclusive, resilient, and capable of fostering social transformation.

The central claim is that **knowledge only travels as far as the systems designed to carry it.** Most learning systems currently attempt to function with one or two of these pillars. The gap between what systems promise and what learners experience is not primarily a funding problem or a training problem — it is a design problem. And design problems can be solved, provided the right elements come together.

## Three pillars the hypothesis rests on

**I — Accessibility as Essential Infrastructure**

Accessibility is the foundational baseline. It focuses on the removal of physical, technological, and attitudinal barriers to ensure equitable participation. Legal frameworks — the Accessible Canada Act (ACA), the AODA, and the ADA — establish the floor. The hypothesis requires moving from reactive retrofitting to proactive, universal design that prevents barriers before they occur. Compliance is the beginning, not the destination.

**II — Disability Justice as Ethical and Political Compass**

While accessibility provides the "how," disability justice provides the "who" and "why." It critiques traditional disability rights frameworks for focusing too narrowly on individual accommodation rather than collective liberation. The core commitments: intersectionality (learners do not live single-issue lives), the leadership of those most impacted, and collective access over individual exception. Disability justice asks whose exclusion is written into the standard itself.

**III — Emergent Learning as Pedagogical Method**

Emergent learning transforms these values into an active, iterative process. It views learning as dynamic, relational, and context-driven — a continuous habit rather than a one-way transfer of information. Rooted in critical theory and Paulo Freire's work, it emphasises collaborative inquiry, iterative experimentation, and the conditions that facilitate emergence rather than the delivery of fixed content. A learning system that cannot adapt cannot include.

## Key claims — hold these when you write

| Claim | What it means in practice |
|-------|--------------------------|
| The three pillars are interdependent | A system with strong accessibility but no disability justice still excludes. A system with disability justice values but no emergent method becomes rigid. All three must be present simultaneously. |
| Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling | WCAG 2.1 and the ACA are necessary grammar. A checklist that passes can still be pedagogically useless or culturally alienating. Write from that position. |
| The gap between policy and practice is a design problem | It is not enough to have a policy. The question is what learners actually experience. The gap is where the inquiry lives. |
| Those most impacted must lead | Not consult, not inform, not validate after the fact. Lead. This changes who is in the room when design decisions are made. |
| Context-driven adaptation is not improvisation | Emergent learning is rigorous. It requires methodological inquiry, real-time data, and reflective practice — not just responsiveness to whatever arises. |
| Knowledge only travels as far as the systems built to carry it | This is the central claim. Every post is testing it. |
