# Weekly Learning Outcomes

What learners should be able to do after each phase — translated into what your writing must deliver

## Phase 1: Foundations & Thesis Discovery (Weeks 1–6)

By the end of Phase 1, learners can identify a high-stakes learning environment where a policy-practice gap exists, draft a statement of intent that names a specific phenomenon, articulate how the three pillars show up (or don't) in their current context, and develop a one-page research proposal that is genuinely responsive rather than fixed.

**Your writing must do**

Lower the threshold of entry in every post. Give readers permission to locate the inquiry in their own real environment — not an idealised one. Establish the vocabulary of the curriculum (the three pillars, practitioner-researcher, policy-practice gap, statement of intent) without jargon. The reader at the end of Week 6 should be able to say: *I know what I'm investigating, why it matters, and how I'm going to begin.*

---

## Phase 2: Literature Review & Theoretical Scaffolding (Weeks 7–12)

By the end of Phase 2, learners can connect their research question to the legal framework (ACA, AODA, ADA), the technical standards (WCAG 2.1), the disability justice compass (intersectionality, leadership of those most impacted), the emergent learning method (Freire, critical theory, context-driven adaptation), and neuroeducational evidence (active learning, spaced repetition, emotional engagement).

**Your writing must do**

Bring theory into contact with practice — not as citation but as living argument. The reader should feel the literature as confirmation of something they've already begun to sense in their own environment, not as external authority they're being asked to accept. A good Phase 2 post creates the feeling: *I knew this was happening in my context. Now I have the language for why.*

---

## Phase 3: Investigation & Methodology (Weeks 13–18)

By the end of Phase 3, learners can defend a mixed-methods approach to a peer, conduct a participatory interview without being extractive, design a survey that doesn't assume a neurotypical or able-bodied experience, and hold ethics as an ongoing relationship rather than a one-time form.

**Your writing must do**

The See sections in Phase 3 should show methodology in action — not just the findings but the choices made. What did the researcher prioritise? What did they refuse to do? What did they hear that they didn't expect? The reader should come away from each Phase 3 post with a more specific sense of what rigorous, ethical field research looks like in a real, imperfect context.

---

## Phase 4: Analysis, Design & Synthesis (Weeks 19–24)

By the end of Phase 4, learners have translated their field findings into a functional learning artifact, applied UDL principles from the first draft rather than retrofitting them later, returned something usable to the community that generated the knowledge, and articulated how their work will continue to adapt as it enters new contexts.

**Your writing must do**

Hold the full arc without summarising it. The synthesis posts are not conclusions — they are arrivals at a horizon. What remains open is as important as what has been resolved. The final post (Week 24, Part 2) should leave the reader feeling that the work is not over, that the field is still being made, and that they are positioned to contribute to it.
