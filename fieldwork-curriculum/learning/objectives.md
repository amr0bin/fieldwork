# Learning Objectives

The five primary objectives the curriculum is designed to achieve — and how each one shapes your writing

---

**1 — Synthesise Infrastructure, Ethics, and Pedagogy**

Learners will integrate the three mutually reinforcing pillars of the Fieldwork framework — accessibility (infrastructure), disability justice (ethics), and emergent learning (pedagogy) — as a single, interdependent system.

The objective requires moving beyond legal compliance to build learning environments that are inclusive, resilient, and capable of fostering social transformation. The failure mode to avoid: treating the pillars as a checklist rather than a synthesis.

*In your writing:* Never let a post live entirely in one pillar. The other two should be audible underneath it. A post about WCAG compliance should carry the implicit question: "compliant for whom?" A post about co-design should carry the implicit question: "what infrastructure makes this possible?"

---

**2 — Transition from Content Consumer to Practitioner-Researcher**

Participants will develop the capacity to formulate and conduct a 24-week investigative research process within a specific high-stakes environment.

This involves narrowing broad curiosities into researchable questions, developing a statement of intent, and utilising mixed-methods approaches to gather data on policy-practice gaps. The transformation is from someone who absorbs ideas about inclusion to someone who generates knowledge about it from inside a real context.

*In your writing:* The posts should model this stance. The "I" of the writing is a practitioner-researcher, not an expert delivering content. Uncertainty, methodology, and positionality are visible — not bracketed.

---

**3 — Implement Proactive Technical and Legal Standards**

Learners will master the application of technical standards (WCAG 2.1) and legal frameworks (ACA, AODA, ADA) as foundational infrastructure — necessary grammar, not final destination.

The objective is to move from reactive retrofitting to proactive, universal design that prevents barriers before they occur. A design that passes a checklist but remains pedagogically useless or culturally alienating has not met this objective.

*In your writing:* Standards are the beginning of a conversation, not the end of one. When a post works with compliance, it must also ask: what does this standard still miss?

---

**4 — Apply Intersectional Disability Justice Frameworks**

Designers will apply an intersectional lens to their work, recognising that learners experience overlapping forms of oppression based on race, gender, class, sexuality, and other identities.

A key outcome is the ability to prioritise collective access and interdependence over individual accommodation, while centring the leadership of those most impacted. This competency requires not just intellectual understanding but structural changes to who is in the room when decisions are made.

*In your writing:* The most impacted learner is never the afterthought. Design for the person facing the most barriers and you serve everyone else too. Design for the average learner and you will exclude.

---

**5 — Design Adaptive Systems using Neuroeducational Principles**

Participants will create context-driven, adaptive learning environments that leverage neuroeducational literacy.

This includes embedding evidence-based practices — active learning, spaced repetition, emotional engagement, collaborative inquiry — to stimulate neural growth and facilitate iterative experimentation in real time.

*In your writing:* Neuroeducational principles are practical tools, not decorative theory. When a post works with brain science, the evidence should feel like confirmation of something the reader has already experienced in practice, not external authority they're being asked to accept.
