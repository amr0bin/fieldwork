# Phase 3 — Investigation & Methodology

**Weeks 13–18 · Goal: Conduct field research to gather qualitative and quantitative data**

Phase 3 is where the inquiry leaves the literature and enters the field. The practitioner-researcher now has a question, a theoretical framework, and a living proposal. This phase asks them to do the methodological work of actually gathering data — while maintaining ethical integrity, genuine curiosity, and a research stance that does not treat participants as subjects.

**What your writing must do in Phase 3**

Show methodology in action. The See sections in Phase 3 should show not just what a researcher found, but how they chose to look, what they refused to do, and what they heard that they didn't expect. The Part 2 activities are genuine research practices, not simulations — readers should come away from each one with actual data about their actual environment.

## Week pages — Phase 3

### Week 13 — What kind of knowledge are you choosing to count as valid evidence?

**Pillar:** Accessibility | **Keywords:** Mixed methods, qualitative research, quantitative research, epistemology, research design

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Most institutional research programmes define valid evidence as measurable, generalisable, and reproducible — which systematically undervalues the specific, contextual, and relational knowledge that lived experience produces. |
| Whose interests | A methodology that counts only what can be measured will find only what was already legible to the people who designed the measurement. |
| What becomes possible | A mixed-methods approach that explicitly values both quantitative patterns and qualitative texture produces evidence that can both describe the scale of a problem and hold the specificity of a life. |

**Part 2 brief:** Defend a mixed-methods approach to a peer. Explain how it will capture both measurable patterns and the lived experience of exclusion. Name explicitly what kind of knowledge you are choosing to count as valid.

### Week 14 — Consultation after decisions are made is not co-design

**Pillar:** Disability Justice | **Keywords:** Co-design, stakeholder engagement, participatory research, design authority, lived experience

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Most "stakeholder consultation" happens after the design framework has been established — which means the people being consulted can affect the details but not the structure. |
| Whose interests | Consultation is for the institution's record. Co-design is for the learner's outcome. The difference is who has authority over the design decisions. |
| What becomes possible | When disabled learners are in the room before design decisions are made — not to validate a design that already exists, but to shape the questions the design is trying to answer — the resulting system is different in kind, not just in degree. |

**Part 2 brief:** Map your stakeholders. Identify who is missing from your organisation's official design and feedback channels. Develop a plan for genuine co-design that involves the most impacted people before design decisions are finalised.

### Week 15 — Listening without extracting is a skill that has to be practised

**Pillar:** Disability Justice | **Keywords:** Participatory interviews, non-extractive research, qualitative data, outlier data, listening practice

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Most research training focuses on how to gather useful data — not on how to be with a person who is sharing their experience of being excluded, in a way that serves them rather than only the research. |
| Whose interests | Extractive research takes what it needs and leaves the participant no better positioned than before. Non-extractive research asks what the participant and their community need from the inquiry. |
| What becomes possible | When a researcher listens for what their structured questions didn't anticipate — the outlier data, the unexpected hesitation, the thing that was said after the recording stopped — they find what the research was not yet asking. |

**Part 2 brief:** Conduct one informal participatory interview in your context. Before you begin, ask what the participant or their community needs from your inquiry process. After, identify the one thing you heard that you didn't expect.

### Week 16 — Numbers reveal patterns — but they can't tell you why

**Pillar:** Emergent Learning | **Keywords:** Survey design, quantitative data, bias in measurement, provocation, follow-up inquiry

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Quantitative data reveals the shape of a problem at scale — it shows where and how often something is happening. It cannot show what it is like to be the person to whom it is happening. |
| Whose interests | A survey designed around a neurotypical or able-bodied experience will produce data that describes that experience and misses the rest. |
| What becomes possible | When quantitative findings are treated as a provocation rather than a conclusion — an invitation to ask why — they open the inquiry rather than closing it. |

**Part 2 brief:** Design a three-question survey for your context. Audit each question for assumptions about a neurotypical or able-bodied experience. Pilot it with your peer group.

### Week 17 — Informed consent is a relationship, not a form

**Pillar:** Disability Justice | **Keywords:** Ongoing consent, confidentiality, anonymity, research ethics, accountability

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Institutional ethics processes treat consent as a one-time event: you get it at the beginning, and then you are covered. |
| Whose interests | A one-time consent form covers the researcher's liability. An ongoing consent relationship covers the participant's experience — including the right to change their mind. |
| What becomes possible | When researchers build renegotiation into their methodology from the start, they can follow unexpected findings without violating the trust the consent was designed to establish. |

**Part 2 brief:** Review your confidentiality and anonymity protocols with a peer who will ask hard questions. Establish a mechanism for renegotiating consent if your research takes an unexpected direction.

---

### Week 18 — What peer inquiry makes visible that solo inquiry cannot

**Pillar:** Emergent Learning | **Keywords:** Collaborative inquiry, cross-pollination, peer research, collective sensemaking, shared patterns

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Solo inquiry produces findings shaped by one researcher's positionality, context, and blind spots — and those blind spots are invisible to the researcher doing the looking. |
| Whose interests | A practitioner-researcher working alone protects their inquiry from challenge. A practitioner-researcher working in peer community exposes their inquiry to the friction that makes it more precise. |
| What becomes possible | When peer researchers share preliminary findings and invite challenge, the patterns that survive across multiple contexts are more robust than any single finding — and the gaps that appear only in one context are more specific and more useful. |

**Part 2 brief:** Host a preliminary findings session with your peer group. Invite those most affected by the system to help analyse the data. Identify the patterns that survive across multiple peer perspectives.
