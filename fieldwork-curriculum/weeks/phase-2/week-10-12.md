# Week 10 — Learning is not a transfer — it is a negotiation

**Pillar:** Emergent Learning

## Keywords

- Emergent learning
- Paulo Freire / banking model of education
- Critical pedagogy
- Context-driven adaptation
- Collaborative inquiry

## Key sentences

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | The banking model of education treats learners as passive recipients of deposits made by experts — and most learning management systems are built on exactly this assumption. |
| Whose interests | A delivery model optimised for content transmission serves the institution's need to document coverage; it does not serve the learner's need to make meaning in their own context. |
| What becomes possible | When learning is designed as a negotiation between the curriculum and the learner's context, what emerges is knowledge the learner can actually use — because it was generated from inside the conditions they are working within. |

## Part 1 brief / Part 2 brief

**Why:** Introduce emergent learning theory through Freire's critique of the banking model and complexity theory's understanding of learning as contextual and adaptive. Distinguish between delivering content and facilitating emergence.

**Try:** Observe one existing learning activity in your environment and identify where learning is already emerging — in the margins, in the questions, in the adaptations learners are making to the official content. Name what conditions made that emergence possible.

# Week 11 — Designing for the neurotypical default excludes by design

**Pillar:** Emergent Learning

## Keywords

- Neuroeducation / neuroplasticity
- Active learning
- Spaced repetition
- Emotional engagement
- Neurotypical assumptions

## Key sentences

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Most learning design is built around how a neurotypical, able-bodied adult processes information — which means it optimises for a cognitive profile that is only one of many, and treats every other profile as requiring accommodation. |
| Whose interests | "Neurotypical by default" design is efficient to produce; it is expensive for everyone who doesn't fit it. |
| What becomes possible | When design starts from neuroeducational evidence — active learning, spaced repetition, emotional engagement, reduced cognitive load — it works better for everyone, and especially for neurodivergent learners who were previously required to work around the design. |

## Part 1 brief / Part 2 brief

**Why:** Introduce neuroeducational literacy as a design tool. Explain what neuroplasticity, active learning, spaced repetition, and emotional engagement actually mean in practice. Show where standard learning design assumes neurotypicality and what the cost of that assumption is.

**Try:** Redesign one session or module using active learning and spaced repetition. Audit your redesign for neurotypical assumptions. Identify what you would need to change for a neurodivergent learner.

# Week 12 — The gap no single pillar can close is where the inquiry begins

**Pillar:** All three

## Keywords

- Research gap
- Synthesis
- Integration failure
- Gap statement
- Cumulative exclusion

## Key sentences

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Current inclusive design literature addresses accessibility, disability justice, and emergent learning as separate fields with separate evidence bases and separate practitioner communities — which means the synthesis the learner needs has never been produced. |
| Whose interests | A gap that exists at the intersection of three fields will not be closed by any one field's practitioners. It requires someone who is accountable to all three simultaneously. |
| What becomes possible | When a practitioner-researcher identifies the synthesis gap — the specific place where the absence of all three pillars working together is causing a specific kind of exclusion — they have found the most generative research question in the curriculum. |

## Part 1 brief / Part 2 brief

**Why:** Bring the literature review to its conclusion. The purpose of reviewing all three pillars is not to master each one — it is to find where their integration is missing. That missing integration is the gap the reader's inquiry is specifically positioned to address.

**Try:** Synthesise your literature scan into a gap statement: where does current inclusive design fall short, specifically because the three pillars are not working together? Stress-test it with a peer.
