# Week 9

**Pillar:** Disability Justice  
**Phase:** Literature Review & Theoretical Scaffolding

## Heading — the provocation

**Disability justice asks who leads — not just who is served.**

## Keywords

- Disability justice framework (Sins Invalid)
- Intersectionality
- Leadership of those most impacted
- Social model of disability
- Collective access

## Key sentences

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Traditional disability rights frameworks centre individual accommodation within existing systems; disability justice asks who designed the system and for whom. |
| Whose interests | A framework that asks "how do we include this person in what already exists?" is a different framework from one that asks "who was excluded in the design of what already exists, and who had the power to make that design?" |
| What becomes possible | When the leadership of those most impacted shapes the design from the beginning, the resulting system does not require the excluded person to fit themselves to the design — the design fits itself to them. |

## Part 1 brief / Part 2 brief

**Why:** Introduce the disability justice framework — Sins Invalid's ten principles, intersectionality, collective access, interdependence. Contrast with the social model of disability and with traditional civil rights frameworks. The primary distinction: disability justice asks about power, not just access.

**Try:** Map the power dynamics in your current environment. Who leads design decisions? Who are those decisions made for? Who is most impacted by them? Where is there overlap, and where is there none?
