# Week 4

**Pillar:** Disability Justice  
**Phase:** Foundations & Thesis Discovery

---

## Heading — the provocation

**A research question that doesn't name who it misses isn't ready yet.**

---

## Three subheadings — the moves

**1. What the system currently does**  
Most research questions in learning design are framed around the system or the average learner: "How can we improve engagement in our LMS?" "What makes onboarding more effective?" These questions are not wrong — they are incomplete. They don't name the people who are most systematically excluded from the existing system, which means the research can produce findings that improve outcomes for people who were already doing reasonably well.

**2. Whose interests that serves**  
A research question framed around the average learner produces findings useful to the average learner. The person who was most excluded before the research was conducted will still be most excluded after. The inquiry reproduced the distribution it was meant to address.

**3. What becomes possible**  
A research question that explicitly names who the current system misses — by identity, condition, or circumstance — produces findings that have to grapple with those specific people. It is harder to do. It produces more generative results.

---

## Part 1 brief

**Why:** Introduce the practitioner habit of naming exclusion explicitly in the research question. Distinguish between questions that improve outcomes generally and questions that centre the most excluded learner specifically.

**See:** A case where narrowing a research question to name a specific excluded population — remote adult literacy learners, neurodivergent students in corporate training, disabled faculty in higher education — produced findings the broad question would have missed.

---

## Part 2 brief

**Try:** Draft three versions of your research question — broad, medium, and specific. The specific version must name the person most excluded by your current system. Pressure-test it with a peer: is it researchable within your real constraints?

**Share:** Bring your three versions to your peer group. Ask: which one would produce findings you couldn't predict in advance?

**Reflect:** What makes it uncomfortable to name exclusion explicitly in a research question? What does that discomfort protect?
