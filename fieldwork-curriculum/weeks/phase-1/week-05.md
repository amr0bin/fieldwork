# Week 5

**Pillar:** Disability Justice  
**Phase:** Foundations & Thesis Discovery

---

## Heading — the provocation

**Ethical constraints are design constraints — not obstacles to them.**

---

## Three subheadings — the moves

**1. What the system currently does**  
Research ethics in most institutional contexts is a front-loaded process: you get consent, you complete the ethics review, and then you proceed. The assumption is that ethics is a gate you pass through, not a relationship you maintain. This works tolerably well when research involves stable populations in stable conditions. It works poorly when you are researching the lived experience of people who have historically been harmed by research conducted in their communities.

**2. Whose interests that serves**  
A one-time consent process protects the researcher's liability, not the participant's ongoing experience. If the research direction shifts, if something unexpected emerges, if the participant's relationship to the inquiry changes — the one-time process has nothing to say about any of that.

**3. What becomes possible**  
When ethics is treated as ongoing practice — a continuous relationship with participants, not a completed transaction — it changes what the research can see. Participants become collaborators rather than subjects. The research direction can shift in response to what emerges without betraying the original consent.

## Key sentences

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | An ethics process designed to protect institutional liability is not the same as an ethics practice designed to protect participants — especially participants from communities with reason to distrust research. |
| Whose interests | One-time consent is a protection for the researcher. Ongoing consent is a commitment to the participant. |
| What becomes possible | When ethics is an ongoing relationship rather than a completed form, the research can change direction without abandoning the people it's supposed to serve. |

---

## Part 1 brief

**Why:** Distinguish between ethics as institutional compliance and ethics as ongoing relational practice. Explain why this distinction matters especially when researching the experiences of people with disabilities or other marginalised identities.

**See:** A case where a researcher had to renegotiate consent mid-project — and what that process made possible that the original consent form couldn't have anticipated.

---

## Part 2 brief

**Try:** Map your available resources, time, and access honestly. Then draft a plan for ongoing informed consent: what will you do if the research direction shifts? How will you maintain transparency with participants throughout?

**Share:** Stress-test your consent plan with a peer who will ask hard questions.

**Reflect:** What would it mean, in your context, to treat research participants from marginalised communities as active partners in the inquiry — not subjects of it?
