# Week 1

**Pillar:** Accessibility  
**Phase:** Foundations & Thesis Discovery

---

## Heading — the provocation

**Your context is not a limitation — it is the research site.**

---

## Three subheadings — the moves

**1. What the system currently does (accessibility / infrastructure)**  
Most learning systems treat accessibility as a compliance destination: something you achieve and then maintain. The policy exists. The checklist is completed. The system is declared accessible. The gap between what the policy says and what learners actually experience remains unexamined — because examining it would require admitting the policy is not enough.

**2. Whose interests that serves (disability justice / ethics)**  
Treating your context as a limitation — too small, too constrained, not a "proper" research environment — is a way of protecting the status quo. It keeps inquiry at the level of the general and theoretical, where no one's actual learners are being missed. The reader's environment is high-stakes precisely because real people are navigating it right now with or without their help.

**3. What becomes possible (emergent learning / method)**  
When practitioners treat their own context as a research site, they become practitioner-researchers. The inquiry is no longer something that happens elsewhere and gets applied here. It is generated from inside the practice, with and for the community the practice is meant to serve.

## Part 1 brief — Why, See

**Why:** Open with the gap that exists in most organisations between official accessibility policy and the reality of what learners navigate daily. Reframe: the reader's specific, imperfect, resource-constrained context is not a limitation on serious inquiry — it is the only site from which honest inquiry can begin. Close with: the gap between policy and practice is not an implementation failure; it is where the design stopped.

**See:** A case where a practitioner in a high-stakes environment — corporate training, post-secondary, community adult literacy — identified a policy-practice gap, named it publicly, and began investigating it from inside. Focus on the choice to locate the inquiry in their own context rather than waiting for ideal conditions.

**Closing question:** What would it mean to treat the gap in your own environment not as evidence of someone's failure, but as the most honest data you have?

---

## Part 2 brief — Try, Share, Reflect

**Try:** Identify one specific accessibility policy in your environment and compare it to the reality of what a learner in that environment actually experiences. Write a one-paragraph statement of intent: name the phenomenon, your point of view, and why this context makes the inquiry urgent.

**Share:** Bring your statement of intent to one person who knows your context well. Ask them: does this name what you've seen too?

**Reflect:** What makes it difficult, in your environment, to treat the gap between policy and practice as a research question rather than a management problem?
