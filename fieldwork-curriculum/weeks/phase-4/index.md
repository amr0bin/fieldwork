# Phase 4 — Analysis, Design & Synthesis

**Weeks 19–24 · Goal: Translate findings into inclusive learning content and finalise the thesis**

Phase 4 is where the inquiry becomes something that can be given back. The practitioner-researcher has gathered data, held it with peers, and found patterns. Now the task is to make meaning from those patterns, design something genuinely useful, and return it — in an accessible format — to the community that generated the knowledge.

**What your writing must do in Phase 4**

Hold the full arc without summarising it. The synthesis posts are not conclusions — they are arrivals at a horizon. What remains open at the end of Week 24 is as important as what has been resolved. The final posts should leave the reader feeling that the work is not over, that the field is still being made, and that they are positioned to contribute to it.

## Week 19 — Thematic analysis that flattens lived experience has not found meaning {#week-19}

**Pillar:** Emergent Learning | **Keywords:** Thematic analysis, qualitative data, meaning-making, lived experience, coding

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Thematic analysis that groups findings into tidy categories produces an account of the data that is legible to the researcher — but may have removed precisely the specificity that made the data generative. |
| Whose interests | A finding that has been smoothed into a theme has been made useful to a general audience and less true to the specific person who produced it. |
| What becomes possible | When thematic analysis preserves the roughness and specificity of lived experience — naming tensions rather than resolving them — the findings can hold what actually happened rather than what would be easier to report. |

**Part 2 brief:** Code your qualitative data with a peer. Compare where you agree and diverge. Identify the theme that you both noticed but that neither of you could fully name — and treat that difficulty as data.

## Week 20 — A local problem and a structural pattern of exclusion are not the same thing {#week-20}

**Pillar:** Disability Justice | **Keywords:** Cross-case analysis, structural patterns, systemic exclusion, local context, transferable findings

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | When practitioners attribute exclusion to local conditions — the specific organisation, the specific leadership, the specific resources — they protect the structural pattern from being named. |
| Whose interests | Framing exclusion as local keeps it manageable and containable. Naming it as structural makes it a design problem — and design problems require systemic responses. |
| What becomes possible | When cross-case analysis reveals that the same pattern of exclusion appears across multiple different contexts, the pattern itself becomes the finding — and no single organisation can attribute it to local conditions. |

**Part 2 brief:** Compare your findings with another group member's research. Identify where the same pattern appears in both. Ask: is this a local problem, or a structural one? What would have to be true for it to be the latter?

## Week 21 — If you start with the average learner, the most excluded learner won't arrive at the end {#week-21}

**Pillar:** All three | **Keywords:** Backward design, end goal, social transformation, UDL, the most marginalised learner

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Backward design is widely understood as starting with the learning objective. Fieldwork extends this: start with what transformation looks like for the most marginalised learner in the system, and design everything backwards from there. |
| Whose interests | A learning outcome defined around the average learner will produce a pathway optimised for the average learner — which is a pathway the most excluded learner will have to work around, as always. |
| What becomes possible | When the destination is defined by the needs of the most marginalised learner, every design decision is tested against the right question: does this help that person get there? |

**Part 2 brief:** Write your end goal statement — define what social transformation or genuine resilience looks like for the most marginalised learner your research has identified. Then work backwards. Does every element of your design serve that destination?

## Week 22 — A prototype that has never met a real person is not a prototype {#week-22}

**Pillar:** Accessibility | **Keywords:** Prototype development, UDL, proactive design, testing with excluded learners, design assumptions

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Most learning prototypes are tested with available, willing participants — which tends to mean participants who are like the designer, and who are therefore unlikely to surface the design assumptions that will fail the excluded learner. |
| Whose interests | A prototype tested only with people who were already included is a prototype that has confirmed it works for people it was already working for. |
| What becomes possible | When a prototype is tested with someone who would typically be excluded by it — before the design is finalised — the invisible assumptions become visible, and the designer has the chance to address them rather than discover them after launch. |

**Part 2 brief:** Build a rough prototype and test it with a real person immediately — specifically someone who would be unlikely to be in the "target audience" as the design team has imagined it. Document what the prototype assumed that the tester surfaced.

## Week 23 — Feedback is part of the design — not a final check on it {#week-23}

**Pillar:** Emergent Learning | **Keywords:** Iteration, feedback, reflection-in-action, reflection-on-action, public redesign

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Most feedback processes are designed to validate a design that is already substantially finished — which means the feedback can affect the surface but not the structure. |
| Whose interests | A feedback process at the end of a design cycle protects the investment already made. A feedback process built into the design cycle protects the learner's experience. |
| What becomes possible | When feedback from original stakeholders — especially those most impacted by the system — is treated as design material rather than quality assurance, the final product is different in kind from what the design team would have produced alone. |

**Part 2 brief:** Run a structured feedback session on your prototype with original stakeholders and peers. Practice reflection-in-action (during the session: what are you noticing?) and reflection-on-action (afterward: what does that mean for the design?). Document the changes you make as a result.

## Week 24 — The work is not done when it is finished — it is done when it is useful {#week-24}

**Pillar:** All three | **Keywords:** Dissemination, accessible format, living resource, community return, ongoing adaptation

**Key sentences:**

| Subheading | Load-bearing claim |
|------------|-------------------|
| What the system does | Most research products are designed to be delivered — to the institution, to the academic record, to the professional community. They are not designed to be used by the people who generated the knowledge. |
| Whose interests | A research product that circulates primarily among professionals who share the researcher's positionality is a product that returns very little to the community that made the knowledge possible. |
| What becomes possible | When the final body of work is returned to the community in an accessible format — usable language, usable structure, usable assumptions — it becomes a living resource rather than an archive. It continues to adapt as it enters new contexts. |

**Part 2 brief:** Finalise your body of work and share it in an accessible format — accessible in language, format, and assumptions. Return something usable to the community that generated the knowledge. Describe how the work will continue to adapt and emerge as it enters contexts outside your control.
