# How to write each week

Your step-by-step process from blank page to published post on [accessiblelearninglabs.com/fieldwork](https://www.accessiblelearninglabs.com/fieldwork)

## Before you begin

Each week produces two posts: Part 1 (Why, See) and Part 2 (Try, Share, Reflect). Both live within the same conceptual territory. Write Part 1 first — it generates the intellectual material Part 2 needs to be useful.

---

## Before you write

**1 — Locate your week on the curriculum**

Open the [24-Week Map](weeks/INDEX.md). Find your current week number and note: the phase (Foundations / Literature / Investigation / Synthesis), the pillar (Accessibility / Disability Justice / Emergent Learning), and the specific topic. Write these at the top of a blank document before you do anything else.

**2 — Identify your heading — the provocation**

This is a single statement or question that names the tension. **Ask yourself:** What assumption about accessible learning does this week challenge? What would a reader assume that this week will complicate? The heading should be arguable — something that could be wrong. Write three options, then choose the one that feels most unsettling in the best way.

**3 — Identify your three to five keywords**

From the [Weekly Structure](foundations/weekly-structure.md) page: every week has conceptual anchors that carry the intellectual weight. Name them before writing. They will appear in the Why section, shape the See section, and echo across both parts. These are the terms that build cumulative understanding across the curriculum.

**4 — Locate yourself in relation to the material**

This is not optional. Fieldwork is practitioner-research — the writing comes from inside a real context, not above it. Before writing, ask: what is the specific gap in *my* environment that makes this week's concept urgent? If you can't answer that, the post will feel like summary rather than inquiry. Write from inside the practice.

---

## Writing Part 1 — Why, See

Use the [Part 1 Template](templates/part-1.md) as your working document.

**5 — Write the Why section (250–350 words)**

Three moves: **(a)** What does the field, institution, or system currently do or assume? **(b)** What concept reframes that? — draw on the Fieldwork hypothesis. **(c)** Close with the load-bearing claim for this section: one sentence a reader could underline. Write from inside the practice. Use "I" where appropriate. Be willing to be uncertain.

**6 — Write the See section (300–400 words)**

Four sub-sections: **Context** — where, who, what conditions. **What they did** — the specific design decisions, not just the outcome. **What changed** — the observable result, including its limits. **What it reveals** — the sentence that connects the case back to the week's concept. This is the sentence a reader will remember.

**7 — Write the closing question**

One genuinely open question — not answerable by the post you just wrote. It should point toward the edge of what is known. What is the field still working out? What might the reader be positioned to discover in their own context?

**8 — Check: load-bearing sentences**

Re-read Part 1. Find the one sentence per sub-heading that a reader could extract and hold as the essential argument. Underline it. If you can't find it, the section needs a revision pass. These are the sentences that build the curriculum's cumulative understanding across 24 weeks.

---

## Writing Part 2 — Try, Share, Reflect

Use the [Part 2 Template](templates/part-2.md). Part 1 must be drafted first.

**9 — Write the threshold sentence**

One sentence that reconnects to last week's concept and signals the shift from understanding to action. Not a recap — a threshold. Something like: *"Last week we looked at why [X]. This week we put it to work."*

**10 — Write the Try section and activity**

One paragraph naming the skill or practice and connecting it directly to the week's concept. Why this activity, why now, why it matters in the reader's context. Then: numbered step-by-step instructions. Write for an early-career practitioner who has never done this before. Assume no institutional resources — adaptable to any context.

**11 — Add the Watch, Share, and Reflect sections**

**Watch:** one sentence introducing the video; 2–3 sentences of context for what the viewer will be able to do afterward. **Share:** one sentence on why this particular concept benefits from being thought through with others; one specific, concrete prompt — not "discuss accessibility" but something grounded enough to start a real conversation across different contexts. **Reflect:** 2–3 sentences — a genuine open question about where the field is heading, something unresolved, something practitioners are still working out.

---

## After writing

**12 — Write your references**

List sources that informed the post (Author, Title, Year + one sentence on why it matters here). Add one or two "worth going deeper with" sources. The reference documents in this project — the comprehensive overview PDFs — are your primary evidence base. Draw from them, don't just cite them.

**13 — Final read: voice check**

Read both posts aloud. Ask: Does this sound like a practitioner-researcher inside a real context, or like an instructor delivering a lesson? Is there any sentence that explains rather than argues? Any place the writing steps above the practice rather than moving inside it? Make those cuts.

**14 — Publish on Substack**

Post Part 1 first. Part 2 follows the following week. Tag the pillar. Include the "Fieldwork: Accessible Learning Labs · Week X, Part Y" label at the top of each post.

---

## A note on voice

Fieldwork speaks from inside the practice, not above it. The "I" of this project is a practitioner-researcher — someone who is genuinely uncertain, genuinely invested in the field, and genuinely accountable to the communities the work is meant to serve. The reader should feel taken seriously, not guided through a lesson. If the writing sounds like a textbook, it has left the practice.
