# Part 1 — Why, See

Complete annotated template for every Part 1 post

**Suggested length**

Why: 250–350 words. See (all four sub-sections): 300–400 words. Total: approximately 600–750 words plus references.

*[One sentence that names what's at stake for the reader personally — not as a summary of the post, but as a reason to keep reading. Write as a practitioner who knows what this costs.]*

## Why

*[2–3 paragraphs. Open with the tension — what the field currently does or assumes, and why that's insufficient. Then introduce the concept that reframes it, drawing on the Fieldwork hypothesis. Close with the load-bearing claim for this section: one sentence a reader could underline. This section should feel like the reader is being taken seriously, not lectured at.]*

*Suggested length: 250–350 words.*

---

## See

*[Introduce the case in one sentence — where, who, what conditions.]*

**Context**  
*[2–4 sentences. Name the environment, the population, the specific challenge or gap they were facing. Be specific enough that readers can locate themselves in relation to it — either "this is my context" or "this is adjacent to my context."]*

**What they did**  
*[2–4 sentences. Describe the design decision, intervention, or practice. Focus on the choices made, not just the outcome. What did they prioritise? What did they refuse to do? What tradeoffs did they make?]*

**What changed**  
*[2–4 sentences. The observable outcome — what was measurably or visibly different afterward. Be honest about the limits of what changed and what didn't. Partial success is more useful than a tidy win.]*

**What it reveals**  
*[2–3 sentences. Connect the case back to the week's concept. What does this example make visible about the hypothesis that a general argument alone could not? This is the sentence a reader will remember.]*

*Suggested length for full See section: 300–400 words.*

## Writing notes for this template

**On the heading:** It should be arguable. Something that could be wrong. Not "WCAG 2.1 matters" (true but obvious) but "A checklist that passes can still exclude" (arguable, specific, and it demands explanation). Write three options and choose the most unsettling.

**On the Why section:** The three moves are: (1) what the field currently does, (2) the concept that reframes it, (3) the load-bearing claim. If any of the three are missing, the section is incomplete. The load-bearing claim is the sentence you'd underline — the one that could be extracted and held independently of the post.

**On the See section:** The "What it reveals" sub-section is the most important. It is not a summary of the case — it is the sentence that connects the specific to the general, the example to the hypothesis. A reader who only reads "What it reveals" should understand both what happened and why it matters for their own context.

**On the closing question:** It should be slightly larger than this week, pointing toward the horizon. If the question can be answered by next week's post, it's not open enough.
